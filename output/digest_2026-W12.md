- ж‘ҳиҰҒеҺҹж–ҮпјҡLiver biochemical and function tests-alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin, prothrombin time, and albumin-are used to screen and identify persons with acute and chronic liver disease. Elevations in ALT and AST reflect hepatocellular injury, with more urgent evaluation required when the INR is prolonged and the bilirubin level elevated. An elevation in alkaline phosphatase reflects a cholestatic process and impaired bile flow. Bilirubin may be elevated with either hepatocellular or cholestatic injury. Albumin, prothrombin time, and bilirubin are tests of hepatic function. Liver tests may also be used in the assessment of prognosis, particularly in persons with cirrhosis, alcohol-associated liver disease, and metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease. Liver test elevations in critically ill patients often reflect severe hepatocellular injury, whereas liver test elevations often indicate hepatotoxicity in persons receiving chemotherapy and immune checkpoint inhibitors. With the availability of many approaches to assessing the diagnosis and severity of liver disease, the role of liver biopsy has become more limited.
